Luminar

a study in vector fields

A vector field hands every point in space an arrow. This page drops thousands of weightless particles into such fields and lets them be carried — through whirlpools and saddle points, around a strange attractor, and through any dataset you drag onto this page. Six specimens follow; after that, the bench is yours.

01The promise of an arrow

A vector field is one of the simplest promises mathematics makes: for every point, an arrow. Weather maps make the promise about wind. Ocean charts make it about current. A magnet makes it about pull. Formally it is just a function — feed it a position (x, y), receive a velocity (dx, dy) — but a formula on paper gives you almost no feeling for what the flow does.

So we ask the field directly. Every dot you see is a massless particle that, sixty times a second, asks “where would you carry me?” and takes a small step along the answer. (Two asks per step, actually — it peeks at the midpoint first, a scheme known as the midpoint rule, which keeps orbits from slowly spiralling where they shouldn't.) Color tracks speed: indigo for the drifters, white-hot for the fastest. The trails are the field made visible — where arrows agree, rivers form.

Nothing here is precomputed or filmed. Every frame, every particle asks anew.

02Six specimens

IThe Vortex

v(x, y) = ω(r) · (−y, x)   ω(r) = 1.6 / (r² + 0.12)

Everything orbits a single eye. Angular speed falls away with distance, so the core turns like a merry-go-round while the rim moves in slow procession — watch the ring of white heat where the two tendencies trade places. This field is pure curl: a paddle wheel dropped anywhere in it would spin. Hurricanes, bathtub drains, and stirred coffee all keep a copy of this plate at their center.

IIThe Dipole

two mirrored vortices at (±0.45, 0), opposite spins

Two eyes with opposite tempers. The left core turns one way, the right the other, and between them they conspire to pump fluid straight through the middle in a fast, narrow jet. This is the secret of the smoke ring: a vortex pair (in 3D, a vortex loop) propels itself forward on the very current it creates. Notice how particles caught in the jet are launched, slow at the fringes, and get folded back around for another pass.

IIIThe Saddle

v(x, y) = (x, −y)

The origin is the loneliest place in this field. Flow rushes toward it along the vertical, flees along the horizontal, and at the exact center the velocity is zero — a stagnation point. Particles approach forever and never quite arrive; the ones that do come close are squeezed thin and flung wide. Dynamics calls this the canonical unstable equilibrium: the mountain pass of flows, where everything almost stops and nothing stays.

IVThe Lorenz Attractor

ẋ = σ(y − x)   ẏ = x(ρ − z) − y   ż = xy − βz
σ = 10, ρ = 28, β = 8/3

In 1963 Edward Lorenz boiled atmospheric convection down to three equations and found weather's confession hiding inside: a two-lobed orbit that never repeats and never escapes. Release two particles a hair's width apart and they part ways within seconds — the butterfly effect, live. The shape they can't leave is a strange attractor. Drag it around; it has no good side, only better ones.

VThe ABC Flow

ẋ = A sin z + C cos y   ẏ = B sin x + A cos z   ż = C sin y + B cos x
A = √3, B = √2, C = 1

The Arnold–Beltrami–Childress flow is a favorite laboratory animal of chaos theory: the velocity field itself is perfectly smooth and periodic — a tidy trigonometric weave — yet the paths of particles through it tangle chaotically. It is proof that turbulent-looking motion needs no turbulent cause. The box wraps like a pattern tile: leave through one face, re-enter through the opposite.

VIThe Fluid

∂v/∂t + (v·∇)v = −∇p   ∇·v = 0

Every other plate is a formula, frozen: ask it twice, get the same answer. This one is the real thing — the Navier–Stokes equations solved live, every frame. Your pointer adds momentum, pressure balances it, and eddies nobody wrote a formula for spin up and remember you after you let go. The fish follow the water. The only plate you can touch.

03Fields from data

Formulas are the exception. Real fields arrive as measurements: anemometer readings, particle-image velocimetry, ocean drifters, RNA-velocity estimates from single-cell biology — a scatter of positions, each with a velocity attached, and no formula in sight.

Luminar's rule for such data is deliberately honest: each particle consults its nearest measurement within a cutoff radius. Inside the radius, the data speaks. Beyond it, silence — particles that wander past the last measurement quietly retire and respawn where measurements exist. Nothing is invented across the gaps (unless you ask: a Smooth toggle blends a handful of neighbours instead of snapping to one — the cutoff still rules).

The format is one CSV row per measurement, with headers or without:

x, y, dx, dy  ·  x, y, z, dx, dy, dz velocity columns may also be named u,v,w or vx,vy,vz — commas, tabs or semicolons, as you like

Drop a file anywhere on this page, or start with ours: download the sample scatter, or load it straight into the observatory below ↓. Your data never leaves the browser — there is no server to send it to.
